Pretty sweet right? First, acknowledgements:

  • Table sorting feature from here.
  • Drive stats from Football Outsiders and current as of week 7 2012.

Ok, so what do these numbers mean? Let’s go left to right to make sure everything is clear. First, as noted above, these are teams’ offensive drive stats. I lifted these numbers from the drive stats page at FBO, which is lacking. First, on FBO each stat has the team’s NFL rank inside the data cell which makes it impossible to sort well, so I got rid of that. You can’t sort this directly on FBO either which is a pain in the neck.

  • Team: obvious.
  • Drives: number of offensive drives team has had so far. This is not exactly the same as possessions since FBO removes kneel-down possessions.
  • Yds/Dr: should be fairly obvious – average yards gained per drive.
  • Pts/Dr: Again, fairly obvious – average points scored per drive.
  • The next five columns describe most of the ways drives can end: with a touchdown, with a punt, and with a turnover. There are columns for fumbles and picks too to breakdown the turnover number. There are some endings that aren’t accounted for here: made field goal, missed field goal, turnover on downs, and end of half or game. The number for each column is basically the percentage of drives that end with that outcome. For example, KC has a TO/Dr number of .288 meaning 28.8% of their drives end with a turnover. That’s bad. Eventually, perhaps once every team has their bye, I’ll combine numbers from ESPN and FBO to get a comprehensive drive picture that will include field goals and turnovers on downs, and by process of elimination, end of half or game.
  • The last two columns are the coolest in my opinion. LOS/Dr is the average starting field position (line of scrimmage) the team has to begin an offensive drive. This is one hidden stat that is rarely discussed but can be hugely important. For example, last year the Niners had both the best average starting offensive field position (just over the 33-yard line), and the best average starting defensive field position, with opponents beginning on average just behind the 24-yard line. A consistent advantage in this area over a season can really add up.
  • DSR – Drive Success Rate. Really, this should be called DSSR for Down Series Success Rate. A down series begins on any first down and ends when the team achieves another first down, scores, or loses possession. Scoring can be a touchdown or a field goal, but only touchdowns matter for this stat, field goals being a less-than-ideal outcome. So DSR measures the precent of down series that end either in another first down or a touchdown. Pretty cool, right?
